01

Overview

CodeLab, a student-run software and design agency at UC Davis, relies on personalized, high-volume email outreach to run recruitment and marketing — but as their audience grew, that workflow fragmented across Airtable, Mailchimp, and Gmail. Simple sends became slow, manual, and easy to get wrong, right at the moments (recruitment cycles) where timing and consistency mattered most.

CodeLab challenged our team of 3 designers and 6 developers to build a tool that could replace that fragmented process, but the team started with no prior experience in automation tools and no clear initial direction from the client.

03

The Problem

CodeLab's communication workflow required manually juggling three disconnected tools:

  • Manual, time-consuming personalization — no way to send personalized emails at scale without doing it by hand
  • Repetitive syncing between Airtable and Mailchimp every time contact data changed
  • Limited collaboration across the team members involved in outreach
  • Overwhelming, cluttered interfaces in the existing tools
  • Audience size restrictions that broke down during CodeLab's largest recruitment pushes

Problem Statement: How might we help CodeLab send personalized, large-scale emails efficiently without relying on restrictive third-party tools?

04

The Design Challenge

How might we replace a fragmented, three-tool workflow with a single system that's flexible enough for power users but still simple enough for non-technical team members?

05

Research

Understanding how people actually send large-scale emails

With no team experience in automation tools, I pushed us toward research grounded in curiosity rather than assumption. I contributed directly to gathering insights, spotting patterns, and translating findings into design direction — informed in part by my own prior experience with CodeLab's broken process.

We used two methods:

  • User Survey (10 participants) — compared expectations across Mailchimp users, other automation-tool users, and manual Gmail workflows
  • Stakeholder Interview — spoke with CodeLab's President to understand the real recruitment communication process

Key findings, and how each one shaped the design:

Finding Design decision
Users need simple, flexible tools Built two editing paths — a full HTML editor for advanced users and a simplified builder for everyone else — instead of one-size-fits-all
Existing tools feel limiting Built a custom Airtable-native tool rather than working within Mailchimp's constraints
Manual emailing doesn't scale Introduced one-click Airtable Sync, replacing manual re-export/re-upload
Workflows are inconsistent across platforms Consolidated list management, templating, and sending into one workflow
Users want more control over templates and personalization Added variable insertion and real-time preview to the HTML editor
Large lists (10k+) are common and need high-volume support Directly shaped my approach to the Contact Groups page — organizing large datasets into selectable, manageable groups

These findings confirmed what my own experience had already flagged: the core issue wasn't the tools themselves; it was the fragmented workflow and lack of structure. This table became the throughline connecting research to every major feature in the final product.

Design Exploration

Designing a structure that could hold thousands of contacts without feeling overwhelming

Early in mid-fidelity, the team had to decide how recipient data should be structured and browsed. I pushed for evaluating this decision directly against the “large lists are common” finding rather than defaulting to a familiar pattern.

✕ Single Long List

  • Simple to build, but became unmanageable once lists reached hundreds or thousands of contacts
  • Gave users no way to segment recipients for different sends
  • Contradicted the research finding that large-scale, high-volume support was a core need

07

Ideation

Turning research insights into early concepts:

  • Low-fidelity exploration — each designer, myself included, sketched navigation, personalization, and sending-flow ideas, responding directly to the “manual emailing doesn't scale” and “workflows are inconsistent” findings
  • Mid-fidelity wireframes — the team aligned on structure (including the Contact Groups decision above), sidebar organization, and page content
  • High-fidelity iteration — focus shifted to component patterns, typography, and extending CodeLab's design system, translating the “personalization control” finding into concrete patterns like variable tags and live previews

Throughout, I made a point of pulling the group back to the research whenever a direction started to drift toward “clever” over “clear” — if an idea didn't map to a specific pain point from the survey or stakeholder interview, I pushed the team to justify it against the research or drop it.

09

Reflection & Takeaways

I learned that leading direction means anchoring decisions in evidence, not intuition.

Automail was my first time working cross-functionally with developers and a project mentor in a lead design capacity, and my first time steering project direction without a clear client brief. Having firsthand experience with the problem gave me a head start, but the bigger lesson was learning to translate that personal insight into research-backed decisions the whole team could stand behind — rather than relying on my own instincts alone.

Takeaways:

  • Designing for technical workflows requires clarity and thoughtful hierarchy
  • Cross-functional collaboration was essential to aligning UI with system behavior
  • Firsthand user experience can be as valuable as formal research for identifying where a workflow breaks down — but it still needs to be validated against real data
  • This project strengthened my ability to both lead design direction and design tools that appear simple on the surface but support complex workflows underneath
