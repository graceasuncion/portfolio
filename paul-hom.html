Paul Hom Asian Clinic Redesign

A refreshed, accessible website for a student-run clinic serving underserved Asian communities.

01

Context

Paul Hom Asian Clinic is a student-run organization providing free healthcare to underserved and uninsured Asian communities. Their existing website hadn't kept pace with the clinic's mission — it looked outdated, was hard to navigate, and didn't convey the professionalism or warmth the organization stands for.

02

Problem

Unclear navigation, inconsistent branding, and poor accessibility created real barriers — for patients trying to find care, volunteers trying to get involved, and donors trying to give. These issues hit hardest for the clinic's older patient demographic, who make up a large share of the people the site needs to serve.

03

Goal

Redesign the website to:

  • Clearly communicate the clinic's mission
  • Improve usability for elderly users
  • Support increased donations and volunteer engagement through stronger branding and design guidelines
04

Research

Auditing the existing site

We audited the original website across three lenses:

  • Branding — outdated visuals, inconsistent identity
  • Accessibility — small text, low contrast, cluttered layouts
  • Design — unclear hierarchy, confusing navigation
Original website screenshots

Competitor analysis

We looked at how other student-run and professional clinics approached similar challenges, including Imani Clinic, Bayanihan Clinic, Shifa Community Clinic, and Stanford Medicine. This surfaced patterns in navigation, readability, and most importantly, how clinics build trust through design.

Competitor analysis visuals
05

Design Process

Early iterations

We started with low-fidelity sketches focused on simplifying navigation and improving readability. My focus was the Committee page — rethinking how to present team roles and information clearly across both desktop and mobile.

Low-fi sketches

Mid-fidelity wireframes

Moving into mid-fi, we introduced interactive elements like cards to break up dense information and improve scannability. I structured the Committee page around a card-based layout so users could browse team roles without feeling overwhelmed.

Mid-fi sketches
06

Design Challenge

Designing for an audience we couldn't directly access: one of the biggest constraints on this project was that many of the clinic's patients are elderly members of the Asian community, and we had no way to interview or test with them directly. Instead, we leaned on indirect research — competitor analysis, heuristic evaluation, and insights from clinic volunteers who work with this population every day.

That constraint shaped our priorities. Without direct testing, we couldn't afford to guess — so readability, contrast, simplified layouts, and clear hierarchy became non-negotiable, not nice-to-haves.

07

Design System

Before moving to hi-fi, we built a lightweight design system to keep the site consistent:

  • Color palette — neutrals with red and gold accents, reflecting warmth, care, and subtle Asian cultural symbolism
  • Typography — a slightly curved sans-serif for an approachable, elegant feel
  • Components — card layouts, buttons, spacing rules, and iconography optimized for readability and accessibility
Design system
08

High-Fidelity Design

With the system in place, we refined the interface using the updated branding and accessibility guidelines. The neutral tones, red and gold accents, and curved sans-serif typeface came together into a visual identity that felt both trustworthy and culturally grounded.

For the Committee page specifically, I focused on strengthening readability and hierarchy through larger typography, consistent spacing, and a clean card-based structure that adapts seamlessly across devices — improving clarity for elderly users without sacrificing a modern look.

Key improvements to the Committee page:

  • Card-based layout for easier scanning and reduced cognitive load
  • Larger, high-contrast typography to support elderly users
  • Consistent spacing and hierarchy for clearer information flow
  • Updated branding — neutral tones, red and gold accents, curved sans-serif typeface
  • Fully responsive design, optimized for both desktop and mobile
09

Final Design — Committee Page (Desktop + Mobile)

I personally designed both the desktop and mobile versions of the Committee page, from mid-fi through final hi-fi. The final page brings the full design system together — updated color palette, curved sans-serif typography, and card-based structure — into an interface that's warm, accessible, and culturally grounded. The layout prioritizes readability, clear hierarchy, and ease of navigation across every device.

10

Reflection

This project taught me how to design with clarity and empathy even when direct access to users is limited. Without the ability to interview the clinic's elderly patient population, I relied on indirect research and volunteer insights to guide decisions around readability, contrast, and layout simplicity.

More broadly, it strengthened my ability to navigate ambiguity, treat accessibility as a core design principle rather than an afterthought, and create a cohesive, culturally sensitive interface that genuinely supports the clinic's mission.
